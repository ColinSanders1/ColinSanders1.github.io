Now that we have a regression equation for predicting how many wins a team will have based on their wOBA, let’s test how accurate it would have been over the past 3 years. The equation is y=257.39x-51.91.

2021

#Example of code for gathering each teams wOBA
data<-ncaa_scrape(257, 2021, type = "batting")
dat.starters=subset(data, AB > 50)
dat.starters$OPS<-c(dat.starters$OBPct + dat.starters$SlgPct)
dat.starters[is.na(dat.starters)] = 0
dat.starters$single=dat.starters$H-(dat.starters$`2B`+ dat.starters$`3B` + dat.starters$HR)
dat.starters$wOBA=c(.69*dat.starters$BB + .722*dat.starters$HBP + 0.888*dat.starters$single + 1.271*dat.starters$`2B` + 1.616*dat.starters$`3B` + 2.101*dat.starters$HR)/(dat.starters$AB + dat.starters$BB + dat.starters$SF + dat.starters$HBP)
dat.starters$kRate<-(dat.starters$K/dat.starters$AB)*100
dat.starters$BBrate<-(dat.starters$BB/dat.starters$AB)*100
wOBA.UGA<-mean(dat.starters$wOBA)

We can see some serious discrepancies here between the model and the actual win total. The predictions for LSU, Arkansas, and Ole Miss were accurate in terms of where they ended up in the standings, but overall it missed in correctly predicting wins. The biggest miss was in ranking Mississippi State, who won 50 games and eventually won the College World Series. The limitation of this model is that it only takes into account hitting stats for each team, so teams who have dominant pitching staffs, like Mississippi State, will be harder to evaluate.
