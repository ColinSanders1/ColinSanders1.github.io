In this analysis, we will look at the the performances of SEC baseball teams over the past 3 seasons. We are going to test the hypothesis that a team’s wOBA is correlated with the number of wins they get during the season. We are also going to test the hypothesis that wOBA is correlated with runs scored per game in order to see how accurate of a measure of offensive production it is.

wOBA, or weight On Base Average, is a stat created by Tom Tango in his book “The Book: Playing the Percentages in Baseball”. Its aims to be a one-stat-fits-all model to measure a player’s offensive production. .400 wOBA is excellent, .320 wOBA is average, and anything below .320 is poor.

#This is how the data was gathered for each team, in the spirit of brevity,
#only one example will be shown.
data<-ncaa_scrape(257, 2021, type = "batting")
dat.starters=subset(data, AB > 50)
#Calculates and adds OPS to the dataframe
dat.starters$OPS<-c(dat.starters$OBPct + dat.starters$SlgPct)
#Replaces 'NA' with a zero
dat.starters[is.na(dat.starters)] = 0
#Calculates and adds singles as a stat so wOBA can be calculated
dat.starters$single=dat.starters$H-(dat.starters$`2B`+ dat.starters$`3B` + dat.starters$HR)
#Calculates and adds wOBA
dat.starters$wOBA=c(.69*dat.starters$BB + .722*dat.starters$HBP + 0.888*dat.starters$single + 1.271*dat.starters$`2B` + 1.616*dat.starters$`3B` + 2.101*dat.starters$HR)/(dat.starters$AB + dat.starters$BB + dat.starters$SF + dat.starters$HBP)
#Calculates and adds K% and BB% to help in lineup construction. 
dat.starters$kRate<-(dat.starters$K/dat.starters$AB)*100
dat.starters$BBrate<-(dat.starters$BB/dat.starters$AB)*100
wOBA.UGA<-mean(dat.starters$wOBA)
